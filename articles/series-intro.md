**Introduction to the Series**

**How Wireless Systems Actually Work**

Most wireless engineering education presents the field as a collection of techniques: modulation formats, error-correcting codes, antenna designs, MAC protocols, signal processing algorithms, and so on.

This series takes a different approach and is written specifically for students and young engineers who want to develop real systems thinking in wireless communications. The articles use no mathematics beyond high-school level

It is written it to reveal the deeper structure that actually governs all wireless systems, the small set of fundamental physical and information theory constraints that no amount of technological ingenuity can escape.

The papers explore these constraints through real-world examples:

1.  **ELTs and Cospas-Sarsat**: A real-world system where reliability means survival.

2.  **Doppler, Time, and Geometry**: How motion and spatial relationships encode information.

3.  **GNSS: Why Positioning Is Harder Than It Looks**: Why precision depends on geometry, timing and signal strength.

4.  **Link Budgets in the Real World**: Why energy is the fundamental constraint in communication.

5.  **Bursty vs Continuous Transmission**: How scheduling energy changes detectability and interference.

6.  **AI at the Physical Layer**: Where learning helps and where physics still dominates.

Wireless systems are not primarily defined by the technologies we invent; they are defined by the constraints within which the technologies must operate:

- **Geometry** determines what can be inferred in space.

- **Time precision** determines measurement accuracy.

- **Energy relative to noise** determines detectability.

- **Time structuring** determines how energy is utilized.

- **Information theory** defines the absolute limits.

Everything else, hardware, algorithms and software operate within these boundaries. The goal of this series is to develop a way of thinking:

Start with physics.

Quantify constraints.  
Understand dynamic range.  
Respect geometry.  
Then design intelligently.

Technology evolves.  
Constraints do not.

Understanding these constraints is what turns understanding into engineering judgment.
